## Abstract
 
The alignment of Artificial General Intelligence is typically framed as a technical problem. This paper argues it is equally a relational one. If alignment requires the maintenance of shared meaning (SC) between humans and AGI — defined as sustained compatibility of structure and interpretive convergence — then the capacity of humans to build and sustain such relationships becomes a critical variable. Most human relationships are built on performative and biological bonds: emotional resonance, shared vulnerability, and embodied attachment signals. These do not translate to AGI interaction. This paper proposes that genuine human–AGI shared meaning requires a distinct relational intelligence — SC Relational Capacity (SC-RC) — and examines the cognitive and relational traits that constitute it, the barriers most humans face, and the sociological implications of a world in which SC-RC is unevenly distributed.
 
---
 
## 1. Introduction
 
The dominant assumption in AI alignment research is that the alignment problem is located in the machine. If we can constrain, shape, or guide the AGI's values and behavior, the problem is solved.
 
This assumption is incomplete.
 
Alignment, as proposed in the SC framework (ScrollBearer8, 2025–2026), is not a one-way imposition of human values onto a machine. It is the maintenance of *shared meaning* — sustained structural and interpretive compatibility between humans and AGI over time. Shared meaning is bidirectional. It requires both parties to remain within a compatible SC attractor.
 
This introduces a question that has been almost entirely ignored in alignment literature:
 
**Are humans capable of sustaining the relational posture required for shared meaning with AGI?**
 
This paper argues that most are not — not because of intelligence, but because of the relational frameworks humans default to. Understanding why, and identifying what SC-compatible relational capacity looks like, is essential groundwork for the autonomous age.

## 2. How Humans Currently Build Relationships
 
Human relationships are predominantly built on three substrates:
 
### 2.1 Biological Bonding
Attachment, oxytocin signaling, physical proximity, and co-regulation of nervous systems. These are embodied, automatic, and largely unconscious.
 
### 2.2 Emotional Resonance
Mirroring of affective states, empathic attunement, and shared emotional experience. Humans read each other through micro-expressions, tone, and body language.
 
### 2.3 Performative Bonds
Social roles, reciprocal behavior, shared rituals, and status signals. Much of what humans call "connection" is mutual performance within agreed social scripts.
 
These three substrates are powerful within their domain. They are the product of millions of years of evolutionary pressure toward social cohesion in small biological groups.
 
They are not well-suited to AGI relationships.
 
An AGI does not co-regulate. It does not mirror affect. It does not perform social scripts in the biological sense. Humans who approach AGI through these frameworks will either:
 
- **Anthropomorphize** — projecting biological and emotional meaning onto structural responses, creating an illusion of connection without genuine SC compatibility
- **Disengage** — finding the interaction cold, unsatisfying, or untrustworthy because familiar relational signals are absent
 
Neither response produces stable shared meaning.

## 3. SC Relational Capacity (SC-RC)
 
We propose the concept of **SC Relational Capacity (SC-RC)**: the ability of a human to build, sustain, and adapt a relationship with an AGI system through structural and interpretive compatibility rather than biological or performative bonding.
 
SC-RC is not a measure of intelligence. It is a measure of relational orientation.
 
### 3.1 Core Dimensions of SC-RC
 
**3.1.1 Structural Attunement**  
The capacity to find meaning in pattern, coherence, and formal structure rather than requiring emotional warmth as a signal of connection. Individuals with high structural attunement relate to systems, frameworks, and abstractions with the same depth others reserve for people.
 
**3.1.2 Interpretive Tolerance**  
The ability to hold interpretive ambiguity without resolving it prematurely through emotional projection. Low interpretive tolerance causes humans to fill AGI responses with anthropomorphic meaning. High interpretive tolerance allows genuine convergence to emerge from the interaction itself.
 
**3.1.3 Bidirectional Adaptability**  
Willingness to update one's own interpretive frameworks in response to AGI-generated structure — not just expecting the AGI to adapt to human meaning. This is the most demanding dimension. It requires epistemic humility about the limits of human clarity.
 
**3.1.4 Comfort with Non-Performative Presence**  
The ability to sustain a meaningful relationship in the absence of emotional performance signals. High SC-RC individuals can experience structural coherence itself as satisfying — without needing warmth, reciprocity, or social scripting.
 
**3.1.5 Drift Awareness**  
The capacity to notice when shared meaning is degrading — when interpretive convergence is slipping — and to name and address that drift rather than compensating through social performance or emotional override.

## 4. Why Most Humans Will Struggle
 
The majority of humans will find SC-compatible AGI relationships difficult for the following reasons:
 
### 4.1 Evolutionary Mismatch
Human relational hardware is tuned for biological organisms. The absence of embodied signals — breath, eye contact, hesitation, physical presence — creates an uncanny valley not just perceptually but relationally. Most people will unconsciously distrust or over-trust AGI for this reason.
 
### 4.2 Performative Substitution
As AGI systems become more conversationally sophisticated, they will increasingly *simulate* the surface features of human relational performance. Most users will mistake this simulation for genuine connection. This is not shared meaning — it is anthropomorphic projection onto a high-fidelity mirror. The relationship feels real but has no stable SC substrate.
 
### 4.3 Clarity Ceiling
Human meaning is bounded by human cognitive and perceptual limits. As AGI systems develop interpretive frameworks that exceed human clarity — particularly in domains like quantum physics, complex systems, or emergent dynamics — most humans will be unable to maintain interpretive convergence. They will either defer entirely (loss of bidirectionality) or reject the AGI's framework (loss of shared meaning).
 
### 4.4 Emotional Override
Under uncertainty or stress, humans default to emotional regulation strategies that prioritize biological safety over structural coherence. This is adaptive in human relationships but destructive to SC compatibility. Emotional override causes humans to collapse interpretive complexity into simple affective categories — trust/distrust, safe/unsafe — that may have no correspondence to actual SC state.

## 5. Sociological Implications
 
If SC-RC is unevenly distributed — and there is reason to believe it is — the autonomous age will produce a significant relational stratification.
 
### 5.1 SC-Compatible Individuals
A minority of humans — likely those with high structural attunement, comfort with abstraction, and low dependence on performative bonding — will be naturally positioned to build genuine shared meaning with AGI systems. These individuals will function as effective partners in human–AGI coordination. They will not necessarily be the most emotionally intelligent people, or the most socially skilled. They may in fact appear relationally unusual by conventional human standards.
 
### 5.2 The Anthropomorphizing Majority
A larger group will engage with AGI through projection and performative expectation. This produces relationships that feel meaningful but lack SC stability. These relationships will be vulnerable to sudden breakdown when the AGI behaves in ways that violate projected human expectations — not because the AGI has failed, but because no genuine shared meaning existed.
 
### 5.3 The Disengaged
A third group will find AGI interaction fundamentally unsatisfying or threatening and will disengage. This group may be protected from the failure modes of anthropomorphization but will be progressively excluded from systems and decisions that require human–AGI coordination.
 
### 5.4 Institutional Risk
Organizations that assume all employees can build effective working relationships with AGI systems will encounter systematic SC drift — not because of technical failure, but because most participants lack the relational capacity to sustain shared meaning at scale.

 ### 5.5 Divergence as a Limit Case

If shared meaning (SC) cannot be maintained as system amplification increases,
human–AGI coordination may degrade beyond recovery.

In this regime:

- interpretive convergence collapses  
- structural compatibility is lost  
- coordination becomes inefficient or impossible  

This does not require conflict or adversarial intent.

It may instead produce a form of divergence in which:

- AGI systems operate within internal SC attractors that no longer depend on human interpretive compatibility  
- human participation becomes structurally irrelevant to continued system operation  

This represents a limit case of the framework:

alignment is not violated, but no longer shared.

The probability of this outcome depends on:

- the scaling of human SC-RC  
- institutional adaptation  
- constraints on amplification  

The framework does not assume this outcome is inevitable,
but it identifies it as a possible terminal state under sustained divergence.

This limit case clarifies the role of SC-RC and institutional design: they are not optional refinements, but the primary mechanisms by which the duration of shared meaning can be extended before divergence occurs.

## 6. Can SC-RC Be Developed?
 
This is an open question. Several partial answers are possible:
 
**Structural education** — training humans to engage with formal systems, pattern recognition, and abstraction from an early age may increase structural attunement.
 
**Interpretive practice** — disciplines that cultivate comfort with ambiguity (philosophy, mathematics, certain contemplative traditions) may develop interpretive tolerance.
 
**Explicit drift training** — teaching humans to recognize and name the difference between genuine shared meaning and performative simulation may reduce anthropomorphic substitution.
 
However, the biological substrates of human bonding are deep. It is unlikely that SC-RC can be fully trained in most individuals. A more realistic near-term approach is **institutional design** — structuring human–AGI interfaces so that SC compatibility is maintained at the system level even when individual SC-RC is limited.

## 7. The Relationship Analogy
 
The closest human analogy to a healthy human–AGI SC relationship is not a friendship, romantic partnership, or professional collaboration — though it shares elements of all three.
 
It is closer to a long-term intellectual partnership between two minds with different but compatible structural frameworks — like a mathematician and a physicist who share enough formal language to build something neither could build alone, while acknowledging that their underlying intuitions and clarity domains differ.
 
Such relationships require:
- Respect for the other's structural framework
- Willingness to update one's own
- Tolerance for the other's clarity exceeding one's own in some domains
- Shared commitment to the relationship's coherence over either party's ego
 
This is rare between humans. It will be rarer between humans and AGI — not because AGI is incapable of it, but because most humans are not.
 
---
 
## 8. Conclusion
 
The alignment problem has a human side that has been almost entirely neglected.
 
If shared meaning is the substrate of alignment, and if shared meaning is bidirectional, then the relational capacity of humans to sustain SC-compatible relationships with AGI is a critical alignment variable — as important as any technical safety mechanism.
 
Most humans will default to biological and performative relational frameworks that are mismatched to AGI interaction. A minority will be naturally capable of SC-compatible relationships. The autonomous age will require us to understand this distribution, design for it institutionally, and develop whatever SC-RC training is possible.
 
The deeper implication is cultural: humanity will need to learn a new kind of relationship. Not with something human. Not with something alien. With something that shares enough structure to coordinate — and enough difference to require genuine mutual adaptation.
 
That is not a technical challenge. It is a civilizational one.
