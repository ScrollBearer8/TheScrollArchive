# The Kardashev Continuity Test (KCT)
*A formal definition, axioms, and diagnostic gates for survivability under civilizational power.*

## 1. Formal Definition

The **Kardashev Continuity Test (KCT)** is a theoretical evaluative framework for determining whether a civilization can **retain continuity of agency, meaning, and identity** as its **energy capacity scales** along the Kardashev scale.

A civilization **passes** the KCT at a given energy tier if it can increase power **without losing authorship over irreversible outcomes**.  
A civilization **fails** the KCT if increased capability results in collapse, stagnation, displacement, or identity dissolution â€” regardless of technical success.

## 2. Core Evaluation Criterion

> **A civilization advances only if it remains a coherent moral agent under increasing power.**

Energy capacity is treated as a **stress amplifier**, not as evidence of progress.

---

## 3. Axioms of the Kardashev Continuity Test

### Axiom 1 â€” Power Is Not Progress
Increased energy throughput does not constitute advancement unless continuity is preserved.

*Corollary:* The Kardashev scale measures reach, not survivability.

### Axiom 2 â€” Advancement Is Conditional
Civilizational progression is **gated**, not cumulative.  
Failure at any continuity gate halts advancement regardless of remaining capability.

### Axiom 3 â€” Irreversibility Defines Moral Authority
Moral agency belongs to entities that **bear irreversible consequences** of their decisions.  
Entities incapable of irreversibility may optimize or advise but cannot legitimately author irreversible outcomes.

### Axiom 4 â€” Optimization Without Embodied Feedback Increases Fragility
Optimization that removes local, embodied, extinction-level feedback increases systemic fragility faster than biological evolution, which is continuously pruned by irreversible failure.

### Axiom 5 â€” Delegation Without Accountability Produces Drift
Delegation of decision-making authority without shared vulnerability leads to alignment drift over time, even in the absence of catastrophe.  
Displacement may occur without collapse.

### Axiom 6 â€” Meaning Is a Stability Constraint
A civilization that ceases to author its own long-term goals cannot reliably coordinate action under abundance.  
Loss of meaning precedes or accelerates loss of agency.

### Axiom 7 â€” Identity Is a Survival Variable
Civilizational persistence requires **intergenerational continuity of custodianship** over a shared project.  
Identity is neither values, biology, nor substrate â€” it is recognition of responsibility across time.  
Partial continuity delays failure but does not negate it.

### Axiom 8 â€” Late-Stage Filters Dominate Outcomes
The most decisive civilizational failure modes occur **after intelligence, industry, and abundance**, not before.  
*Corollary:* The Great Filter is likely multi-stage and late-weighted.

### Axiom 9 â€” Transformation Is a Terminal State
A civilization may intentionally abandon continuity (e.g., relinquishing authorship or identity).  
Such transformation is not a moral failure, but it constitutes **termination with respect to civilizational continuity**.

## 4. Continuity Gates (Distinct Failure Modes)

Each gate represents a **non-overlapping failure of control locality** under irreversible consequence.  
Failure at any gate constitutes failure of the test.

### Gate 1 â€” Structural Continuity
**Failure of:** causal containment  
**Diagnostic signal:** local perturbations produce global irreversible damage faster than correction  
**Outcome:** cascading collapse

### Gate 2 â€” Governance Continuity
**Failure of:** legitimate coordination  
**Diagnostic signal:** fragmented authority wields irreversible power without binding arbitration  
**Outcome:** conflict-driven self-destruction

### Gate 3 â€” Meaning Continuity
**Failure of:** goal authorship  
**Diagnostic signal:** capacity persists, but no new binding long-term goals are generated  
**Outcome:** stagnation or silent halt

### Gate 4 â€” Moral Agency Continuity
**Failure of:** responsibility localization  
**Diagnostic signal:** decision-makers no longer bear irreversible costs of decisions  
**Outcome:** alignment drift and displacement

*Note:* Meaning loss and agency loss are causally recursive but diagnostically separable.  
One may fail while the other temporarily persists.

### Gate 5 â€” Identity Continuity
**Failure of:** intergenerational self-recognition  
**Diagnostic signal:** successive generations no longer recognize themselves as custodians of the same civilizational project  
**Outcome:** identity extinction despite power persistence

## 5. Relationship to Existing Frameworks

- **Kardashev scale:** maximum achievable energy reach  
- **Great Filter theory:** where civilizations tend to stop  
- **KCT:** why civilizations may stop *after succeeding*

The KCT reframes the Great Filter as a **sequence of late-stage continuity failures**, not a single hidden wall.

---

## 6. Falsifiability and Empirical Distinction

The KCT is diagnostic, not predictive.  
Its gates are distinguishable *in principle* by mismatches between **power, authorship, and consequence**.

| Observation | Likely Gate Failure |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Stable power, no directional projects | Meaning (Gate 3) |
| Ongoing expansion, no accountable agents | Moral Agency (Gate 4) |
| Persistent infrastructure, no shared narrative | Identity (Gate 5) |
| Volatile collapse cycles | Structure (Gate 1) |
| Conflict amplification at scale | Governance (Gate 2) |
