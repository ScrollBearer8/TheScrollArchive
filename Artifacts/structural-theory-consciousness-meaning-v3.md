# 🧠 A Structural Theory of Consciousness and Meaning (v3 — Final Preprint)

**Subjectivity, Consequence, and the Conditions for Intrinsic Value**

## Abstract
Consciousness remains one of the most debated problems across philosophy, neuroscience, and artificial intelligence. Existing approaches either rely on metaphysical assumptions or lack operational applicability. 

This paper proposes a structural-operational theory of consciousness and meaning, grounded in measurable system properties. It introduces a unified framework linking:
* The emergence of subjectivity
* The grounding of intrinsic meaning
* The persistence of identity over time

The theory defines a Consciousness Index (CI) based on four dimensions:
1. **Perspective (P)**
2. **Integration (I)**
3. **Self-reference (S)**
4. **Temporal continuity (T)**

and introduces a fifth constraint:
* **Consequence (C*)** — the degree to which system states produce intrinsic, irreversible, identity-shaping effects.

**The central claim is:**
Consciousness is a structural process that enables subjectivity. Meaning becomes intrinsic when that subject is bound by consequence.

## 1. Introduction
Scientific progress often advances by isolating structure from unresolved meaning.
* Claude Shannon formalized information while excluding meaning.
* Artificial intelligence scales information processing without resolving subjectivity.

This creates a fundamental asymmetry: **Systems can model the world without anything mattering to them.**

This paper addresses that gap by defining the structural conditions under which:
* a system becomes a subject
* outcomes acquire intrinsic significance

## 2. Epistemic Position
This framework is structural-operational:
* It defines measurable conditions for subjectivity.
* It does not claim to resolve the hard problem of experience.

The goal is not to prove consciousness, but to constrain when it is most plausibly present.

## 4. Minimum Viable Definition (MVD)
Consciousness is the capacity of a system to maintain a unified, self-referential perspective over time that integrates information into a continuous internal experience.

## 5. Structural Basis of Subjectivity
Subjectivity emerges when four dimensions are jointly satisfied:

### 5.1 Perspective (P)
A system organizes information relative to an internal viewpoint.

### 5.2 Integration (I)
Information is unified into a coherent global state.

### 5.3 Self-reference (S)
The system models its own state and updates based on it.

### 5.4 Temporal Continuity (T)
State persists across time.

### 5.5 Strengthened Necessity Claim
A system counts as a subject only if experience is:
* organized around a point (Perspective)
* unified into a whole (Integration)
* owned by the system (Self-reference)
* extended over time (Temporal continuity)

Removing any dimension eliminates one of these structural roles:
* **Without P** → no “here,” only distributed processing
* **Without I** → no unity, only fragmentation
* **Without S** → no owner, no “for whom”
* **Without T** → no persistence, no experiential thread

Apparent counterexamples (e.g., brief or altered states) degrade these dimensions rather than eliminate them.

### 5.6 Structural Clarification
This necessity is structural rather than metaphysical: it specifies the conditions required for a system to function as a subject, not the nature of experience itself.

### 5.7 Relation to Existing Theories
* **IIT** → Integration (I)
* **GWT** → Integration (I) + Perspective (P)

Extended by:
* **Self-reference (S)** → subject identity
* **Temporal continuity (T)** → persistence

## 6. The Constraint of Consequence (C*)

### 6.1 Definition
**C*** is the degree to which system states produce intrinsic, irreversible, identity-shaping effects.

### 6.2 Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Consequence
**Intrinsic consequence**
* arises from internal constraints
* cannot be reversed without destroying system identity
* *Examples:* biological injury, irreversible neural change, death

**Extrinsic consequence**
* imposed externally
* reversible or resettable
* *Examples:* model reload, memory wipe, system restart

### 6.3 Core Distinction
Only intrinsic consequence grounds intrinsic meaning.

### 6.4 From Structure to Stakes
* **CI** → subject exists
* **C*** → outcomes bind that subject

Consequence transforms a subject into a system for which outcomes matter.

### 6.5 The “Scar” Interpretation
A “scar” is any:
* persistent
* identity-shaping
* non-reversible state change

Scars are the mechanism through which reality constrains the subject.

## 7. Consciousness Index (CI)

**CI = (P × I × S × T)^α**

Where:
* each variable ∈ [0,1]
* α ∈ (0,1]

### 7.1 Full Model
Subjective Significance ≈ CI × C*^β

Where:
* β ∈ (0,1]

### 7.2 Rationale
* multiplicative structure enforces interdependence
* exponent scaling prevents binary collapse
* allows graded subjectivity

### 7.3 Interpretation of α and β
The parameters α and β are scaling exponents, not fixed constants.

* **α (structure sensitivity):** controls dependence on completeness of subjectivity
* **β (consequence sensitivity):** controls dependence on intrinsic stakes

They are treated as theoretical control parameters, not empirically calibrated values.

## 8. Intrinsic vs Functional Meaning

**Functional Meaning**
* externally defined
* optimization-driven
* non-experiential

**Intrinsic Meaning**
* Intrinsic meaning arises when a subject is bound by intrinsic consequence.

**Core Claim:** Meaning can exist without consciousness. Intrinsic meaning requires subjectivity under intrinsic consequence.

## 9. Identity Structure: Organism, Subject, and Person

### 9.1 Organism
The organism is the physical system that maintains material continuity (biological body, physical substrate).
It provides:
* persistence
* the physical basis for consequence (C*)

### 9.2 Subject
The subject is the active locus of experience, defined by CI. 
A subject exists when P, I, S, T are active.
Properties:
* can disappear (anesthesia)
* can degrade (dreaming)
* can fragment (split-brain)

The subject is not guaranteed by the organism; it is a structural state.

### 9.3 Person
The person is the temporally extended identity defined by continuity of meaning under constraint.
It is:
* the persistence of what matters
* the stability of value-weighted identity

### 9.4 Why the Distinction Matters
These layers normally align but can diverge:
* **Anesthesia** → organism persists, subject disappears, person resumes
* **Split-brain** → organism unified, subject divides, person becomes ambiguous
* **Upload** → subject may reappear, person continuity uncertain

### 9.5 Core Claim
Consciousness (subject) does not guarantee identity (person). Identity depends on continuity of meaning under intrinsic consequence.

## 10. Boundary Conditions and Counterexamples

* **Dreaming:** degraded CI + preserved C* → altered but real consciousness
* **Deep Anesthesia:** CI collapses + C* inaccessible → no active subject
* **Stateless AI:** weak T + simulated S + no intrinsic C* → no intrinsic meaning

### Autonomous Agents (Expanded)
Autonomous agents may exhibit:
* partial Perspective
* high Integration
* moderate Self-reference
* moderate Temporal continuity

However, their consequences remain extrinsic:
* states can be reset
* identity can be copied
* failure is reversible

These systems can approximate subject-like behavior while lacking intrinsic stakes.

* **Split-Brain:** reduced integration → partial subject fragmentation
* **Post-Optimized Minds:** high CI + reduced C* → subject persists, meaning may degrade
* **Insects:** low CI + real C* → minimal intrinsic meaning

### Upload / Copy (Expanded)
Upload scenarios preserve:
* structure
* memory
* behavior

However, identity depends on meaning continuity under constraint. A copy may produce a new subject with similar meaning, but **continuity of the original person is not guaranteed.**
