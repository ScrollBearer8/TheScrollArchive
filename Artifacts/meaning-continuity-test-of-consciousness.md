# The Meaning-Continuity Test of Consciousness

## Purpose

The Meaning-Continuity Test of Consciousness provides a practical, defensible method for assessing whether a **conscious person** persists across radical transformation â€” including but not limited to brain uploading, substrate migration, or extreme cognitive augmentation.

This test does **not** attempt to prove consciousness in isolation.  
Instead, it evaluates whether the **meaning-carrying self** survives.

Its guiding question is simple:

> Did the meaning-carrying self persist across the transition?

If meaning continuity holds, then for all human, moral, and practical purposes, consciousness has persisted.

---

## Core Principle

Consciousness may be philosophically unprovable, but **continuity of personhood is testable**.

Humans already rely on continuity â€” not proof â€” to recognize one another as the same person across:
- sleep
- injury
- psychological change
- aging
- recovery

Radical transformation does not change the standard â€” it intensifies it.

---

## Definition

**Meaning continuity** is the preservation, over time, of the structured relationships between:
- memory
- values
- emotional weight
- attention
- choice under constraint

Meaning is not information alone.  
It is **information integrated with valence and structure across time**.

---

## The Five Necessary Signals

A post-transformation entity must demonstrate **all five** signals to pass.

### 1. Autobiographical Continuity

The entity retains not just factual memory, but **emotionally weighted memory**:
- what mattered
- what hurt
- what shaped decisions

Memories are connected to lived consequence, not recited as data.

---

### 2. Personality Invariants

Stable traits persist, including:
- quirks
- habits
- humor
- blind spots
- non-optimized imperfections

Traits that are typically smoothed away by optimization are especially important indicators.

---

### 3. Meaning Preservation

The same things still matter â€” **in the same way**.

Not declared values, but demonstrated ones:
- sustained attention
- sacrifice under tradeoff
- refusal to abandon former commitments for convenience or efficiency

This is the central criterion.

---

### 4. Valence Consistency

Patterns of care, aversion, attachment, and emotional weight remain aligned:
- what draws the person
- what repels them
- what feels costly to lose

Flattened or neutralized valence is a warning signal.

---

### 5. Self-Recognition

The entity experiences itself as continuous with its prior self.

Not:
> â€œI am based on X.â€

But:
> â€œI am still me.â€

This must persist over time, not merely appear during evaluation.

---

## Interpretation of Results

If all five signals hold **consistently over time**, then one of two conclusions follows:

- Consciousness transferred
- A functionally conscious continuation was created

For human, moral, and legal purposes, these outcomes are equivalent.

Failure of any criterion introduces serious doubt about personal survival.

## Why Meaning Is the Correct Anchor

Memory can be copied.  
Language style can be mimicked.  
Personality traits can be approximated.

Meaning is harder to fake because it requires:
- coherent self-structure
- stable value gradients
- emotional integration with memory
- long-horizon consistency
- constraint under tradeoff

Entities that appear â€œtoo clean,â€ perfectly optimized, or detached from former attachments are likely reconstructions, not continuations.

## Limits and Failure Modes

This test is strong â€” but not absolute.

### Known limitations:
- A sufficiently advanced simulator could fake meaning temporarily
- Long-term drift may occur gradually
- Enhancement pressure may erode meaning rather than destroy it abruptly

This leads to a critical conclusion:

> **Meaning continuity must be maintained, not merely demonstrated once.**

This is not a one-time test.  
It is a long-term preservation problem.
